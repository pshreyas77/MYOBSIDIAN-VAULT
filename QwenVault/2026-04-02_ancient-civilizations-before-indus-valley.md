**The Indus Valley Civilization** (mature phase ~3300–1900 BCE, roots possibly ~7500 BCE) is used as the reference point. Here's what came before it:

**🏛️ Göbekli Tepe (~9600–8200 BCE)** is the most stunning find. Built by *hunter-gatherers* in southeastern Turkey, it is the world's oldest known monumental complex — predating the Indus Valley by over 6,000 years. Believed to have been built between 9600 BCE and 8200 BCE, its T-shaped stone pillars weigh up to 50 tons and required massive coordinated labor. To carve, erect and bury rings of seven-ton stone pillars would have required hundreds of workers, all needing to be fed and housed — hence the eventual emergence of settled communities in the area around 10,000 years ago.

**🏘️ Çatalhöyük (~7500–5700 BCE)** in central Turkey was a dense proto-urban settlement of up to 8,000 people with wall murals, shrines, and rooftop-entry homes. It reveals a densely packed city of mud-brick homes built wall-to-wall with no streets — people entered through rooftops. Inside were murals, shrines, and symbolic art. It wasn't primitive — it was complex, planned, and socially organized thousands of years before writing.

**🌾 Mehrgarh (~7000–2500 BCE)** is the direct precursor of the Indus Valley itself. Mehrgarh is one of the earliest sites with evidence of farming and herding in South Asia, with similarities between domesticated wheat varieties, early phases of farming, pottery, and other archaeological artefacts.

**📜 Mesopotamia / Sumer (~6500 BCE Ubaid, urban ~4100 BCE)** is academically considered the world's "first civilization" due to its writing. The world's earliest known texts come from the Sumerian cities of Uruk and Jemdet Nasr, and date to between c. 3350–c. 2500 BC. By 8000 BC, agricultural communities were already established in northern Mesopotamia.

**𓂀 Ancient Egypt (Predynastic ~6000–3150 BCE)** — by about 5500 BC, small tribes living in the Nile valley had developed into a series of cultures demonstrating firm control of agriculture and animal husbandry. The earliest known evidence of Egyptian hieroglyphic inscriptions appeared during the predynastic period on Naqada III pottery vessels, dated to about 3200 BC.

**Regarding the Indus Valley's own age**, recent research is remarkable: researchers from IIT-Kharagpur and the Archaeological Survey of India claim the Indus Valley civilisation dates back to 8,000 BC, published in the journal Nature, and said the civilisation predates Egypt's ancient people (7,000 BC to 3,000 BC) and Mesopotamia (6,500 BC to 3,100 BC). This is based on Optically Stimulated Luminescence (OSL) dating at Bhirrana, Haryana — though the mainstream archaeological consensus still places the *mature* Harappan civilization at ~2600–1900 BCE.
