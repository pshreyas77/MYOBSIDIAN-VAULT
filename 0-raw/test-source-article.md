## Raw Content

The concept of "digital gardens" has gained traction since 2020 as an alternative to chronological blogging. Unlike social media feeds, digital gardens emphasize:

1. **Evergreen content** — Notes that grow and change over time rather than static posts
2. **Combinatorial growth** — Ideas connect in non-linear ways, creating emergent patterns
3. **Public thinking** — The garden shows work-in-progress, not just polished conclusions

Vanessa Groot (2024) defines a digital garden as "a personal knowledge base that is publicly accessible and evolves through continuous refinement." This contrasts with traditional blogs where posts are time-stamped and rarely updated.

Key technical requirements for digital gardens:
- Bidirectional links (backlinks)
- Graph visualization
- Version history for each note
- Search by concept, not just date

Maggie Appleton's 2025 research found that 67% of digital gardeners report improved retention of complex concepts compared to linear note-taking.

The movement traces back to Ward Cunningham's WikiWikiWeb (1995) and Vannevar Bush's memex concept (1945), but modern implementations leverage:
- Obsidian.md for local-first knowledge management
- Roam Research for networked thought
- Logseq for outline-based gardens

**Critical claim:** Digital gardens work best when users commit to daily refinement sessions of 15-20 minutes, according to a 2026 study by the Personal Knowledge Management Institute.
