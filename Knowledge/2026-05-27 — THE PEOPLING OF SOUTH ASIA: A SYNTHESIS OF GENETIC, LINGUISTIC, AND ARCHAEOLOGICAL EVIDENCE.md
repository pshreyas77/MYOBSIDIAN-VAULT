## Key Findings
1. Steppe pastoralist ancestry (Yamnaya-related) arrived ~2000-1500 BCE, not ~1200 BCE
2. Two primary ancestral components:
   - Ancient Ancestral South Indians (AASI): lineage splitting ~12,000 years ago
   - Iranian farmer-related ancestry: entered ~4700-3000 BCE
3. AASI diverged from East Eurasians ~12,000 years ago, deeper split than Papuan/Andamanese
4. Indus Valley Civilization (IVC) formed ~6000-4700 BCE from Iranian farmer + AASI admixture
5. No significant steppe ancestry in IVC; Vedic Aryans postdated IVC collapse
6. Indo-Aryan languages ~3.5-4.0 kya; Dravidian languages ~4.5-5.5 kya
7. Brahui is a relic Dravidian language, not a recent migration
8. IVC likely spoke a Dravidian language based on linguistic evidence
9. Vedic Sanskrit shows substrate influence from lost IVC language (retroflex sounds)
10. Cultural continuity: IVC → Vedic (fire altars, water management, urban planning, craftsmanship)
11. Vedic Indo-Aryans were not nomads; had cities, agriculture, metallurgy
12. "Indigenous Aryan" theory (OIT) lacks evidence: no steppe ancestry in IVC, no horse/chariot in IVC
13. Settlement patterns: Early Vedic (rural, pastoral) → Later Vedic (urban, complex society)
14. Linguistic evidence: Sanskrit closest to Lithuanian/Old Irish, not to Avestan
15. Tamil grammar oldest extant (Tolkāppiyam ~1st-5th century CE)
16. Sanskrit and Tamil influenced each other: Sanskrit loanwords in Tamil, Dravidian syntax in Sanskrit
17. South Indian temple architecture blends Nagara and Dravidian styles
18. Indian civilization formed through integration, not replacement or imitation
19. Tribal societies exist outside caste system (e.g., Northeastern states)
20. Vedic varna system emerged ~1000-900 BCE, not rigid in early Vedic period
21. Indian civilization: continuous, indigenous development incorporating migrations

## Conclusion
Indian civilization is older, deeper, and more complex than colonial narratives suggest. Result of 12,000+ years of integration between indigenous populations and migrants.
