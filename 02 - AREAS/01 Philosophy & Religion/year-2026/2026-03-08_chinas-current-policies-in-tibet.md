## China's Actions in Tibet – Current Situation (2025–2026)

### 🏠 Forced Relocation & Displacement
Chinese authorities have used coercive tactics and misleading information to carry out the mass relocation of rural Tibetan villages. Since 2016, the project displaced more than 500 whole villages with some 140,000 residents, with much larger numbers displaced through individual household relocations. More broadly, between 2000 and 2025, approximately 3.36 million Tibetans have been affected by government programmes requiring nomads to become sedentary, with around 930,000 rural Tibetans relocated through either whole-village or individual household programs.

### 🏫 Cultural & Language Suppression
Authorities continued their campaign to close privately run Tibetan schools while forcibly enrolling ethnic Tibetan children in residential schools that feature political indoctrination and efforts at assimilation into a regime-approved version of Chinese cultural and linguistic identity. A language rights advocate named Gonpo Namgyal died from torture while in custody, highlighting the dangers of cultural activism.

### ⛪ Religious Control
China continued to restrict and seek to control the religious practices of Tibetans, including by asserting control over the selection of Tibetan Buddhist religious leaders. Chinese authorities continue to penalize Tibetans for expressions of reverence for the Dalai Lama, including through harassment and surveillance, detention, and imprisonment.

### 📡 Surveillance & Repression
U.S. congressional leaders urged the imposition of export controls on technology used by China's Public Security Bureaus in Tibet to collect biometric data, which is used by police for political identification and racial profiling. Authorities continue to impose strict controls on movement, religion, language, and access to information.

### 👷 Forced Labour
UN experts expressed deep concern about a persistent pattern of alleged state-imposed forced labour involving ethnic minorities, including Tibetans. The coercive elements are described as so severe that they may amount to forcible transfer and/or enslavement as a crime against humanity.

### 🏗️ Infrastructure & Demographics
China has planned massive infrastructure investments in Tibet — railways, highways, and airports — which have facilitated an influx of Chinese migrants into Tibet, significantly altering the demographic composition of urban areas like Lhasa. At the same time, Tibetan populations have been dislocated, with China's aim being to weaken traditional social structures and erode Tibetan cultural identity.

The U.S. Congressional-Executive Commission on China noted no interest or progress on the part of China toward resuming formal negotiations with the Dalai Lama or his representatives. The last dialogue took place in 2010.

## Civilian Deaths in Tibet Under Chinese Rule

### 📅 Historical Mass Killings (1950s–1960s)
During the land reform period, communist agitators designated "landlords" — sometimes arbitrarily chosen — for public humiliation in so-called "struggle sessions", torture, maiming, and even death. During the fighting in that era, tens of thousands of Tibetans were killed.

According to one account: "In Golok area, many people were killed and their dead bodies were rolled down the hill into a big ditch. The soldiers told the family members that they should celebrate since the rebels had been wiped out. They were even forced to dance on the dead bodies. Soon after, they were also massacred with machine guns."

### 📅 1959 Uprising
According to a secret People's Liberation Army document purportedly captured by Tibetan guerrillas, 87,000 deaths were recorded in Lhasa between March 1959 and September 1960.

### 📅 2008 Crackdown (Most Documented Modern Case)
At least 80 people were killed on 14 March 2008. An estimated 80 bodies were seen piled near the Lhasa Public Security Bureau Office on 15 March 2008. Indiscriminate firing into protesting crowds resulting in Tibetan deaths was confirmed in Lhasa, Ngaba, and Karze.

After the 2008 protests, many detained individuals were subjected to extrajudicial punishments such as severe beatings and deprivation of food, water, and sleep for long periods. In some cases, detainees sustained broken bones and other serious injuries at the hands of security forces.

### 📅 Deaths in Custody (Ongoing)
Exile groups report that Tibetans in China are subjected to disappearances and torture, including electric shocks, cold exposure, and severe beatings. Hundreds have been killed in crackdowns, and thousands are arbitrarily detained.

### 🔢 Overall Death Toll Estimates
The 1.2 million figure cited by the Tibetan exile government has been described by serious observers, including Human Rights Watch, as unverifiable. However, scholars acknowledge there is a "gory and horrible truth" behind the accounts, and that China's refusal to open its archives speaks volumes about what those records may reveal.

**In summary:** Yes, civilians have been killed — documented most clearly during the 1959 uprising and the 2008 crackdown. The full death toll remains disputed because China heavily restricts independent access to Tibet and controls all information coming out of the region.

## Tibet: Deaths & Killings in the Last 5 Years

### ⚠️ Important Context First
China completely restricts independent journalists and investigators from entering Tibet. This means **many deaths go unreported or unverified**. What we know comes from exile groups, Radio Free Asia, and U.S. State Department reports.

---

### 💀 Deaths in Custody / Prison (2020–2025)

This is the most documented form of killing in recent years — people arrested for minor acts who died from torture or neglect:

Buddhist monk **Phende Gyaltsen** died in prison in January 2023, less than a year after his arrest in March 2022. He was reportedly arrested for working on the renovation of a Tibetan Buddhist monastery. Authorities blocked public access to his body and prohibited his family from performing last rites. He was reportedly healthy before his imprisonment.

Reports from released prisoners indicate some had **permanent disabilities or were in extremely poor health** due to harsh treatment — including isolation in small cells for months, deprivation of sleep, sunlight, and food.

A June 2025 report by the Central Tibetan Administration stated that since 2000, a large number of Tibetans have **lost their lives in prison, shortly after release, or in hospitals and their own homes** — deaths described as the product of systematic torture, long-term denial of medical care, and inhumane prison conditions.

---

### 🎵 Recent Arrests Risking Death (2024–2025)

Tibetan singer **Ah Sang** (also known as Tsugte) remained in Chinese custody as of October 2025 after being arrested in July for **performing a song honoring the Dalai Lama's 90th birthday**.

In March 2024, Tibetan monk **Jampa Choephel** was arrested for sharing a speech by the Dalai Lama online and was sentenced to 18 months in prison.

---

### 📋 U.S. State Department Findings (2022–2023)

The U.S. State Department's 2022 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices confirmed **unlawful or arbitrary killings by the government**, disappearances, and torture in Tibet — rating Tibet's human rights situation as among the worst on Earth, worse than even North Korea.

---

### 🔍 Why We Don't Have Full Numbers

China tightly controls all information coming out of Tibet. There are **no confirmed mass shootings or open military killings** documented in the last 5 years — unlike in 2008. However, the deaths occurring through torture in custody, denial of medical care, and systematic repression are described by human rights organizations as **state-sponsored killing by other means**.

In short: people are dying — just more quietly, inside prisons, away from cameras.

- China's People's Liberation Army invaded Tibet
- Thousands of Tibetan soldiers and civilians were killed in the initial conquest

#### 📅 1956–1959 — Uprising & Brutal Crackdown
- Tibetans rose up against Chinese rule
- Chinese military responded with **machine guns, artillery, and bombers**
- Monasteries were shelled with people still inside
- One PLA internal document confirmed **87,000 Tibetans killed** in Lhasa alone between 1959–1960
- The Dalai Lama fled to India in 1959, never to return

#### 📅 1966–1976 — Cultural Revolution
- One of the darkest periods for Tibet
- Over **6,000 monasteries were destroyed**, many with monks still inside
- Tibetans were publicly tortured and executed in "struggle sessions"
- Famine caused by forced collectivization killed tens of thousands more
- Monks and nuns were forced to break vows publicly or face death

#### 📅 1987–1989 — Protests & Shootings
- Tibetans protested for independence
- Chinese police and military **opened fire on unarmed crowds**
- Hundreds killed, thousands arrested and tortured
- Martial law was imposed on Lhasa in 1989

#### 📅 2008 — Biggest Modern Crackdown
- Tibetans across the plateau rose up in the largest protest since 1959
- Chinese security forces **shot into crowds**
- At least **80–200 civilians confirmed killed** (exact numbers blocked by China)
- Over 6,000 arrested; many tortured or killed in custody
- China deployed military troops and armored vehicles into Lhasa

- Since 2009, **over 160 Tibetans** have set themselves on fire in protest against Chinese rule

- Most called for the return of the Dalai Lama and Tibetan freedom before dying
- China banned reporting on these incidents and arrested family members of those who self-immolated

## Deaths & Killings in Tibet Under Chinese Rule

### 📊 Total Estimated Deaths (Overall)
The Tibetan exile government estimates that **1.2 million Tibetans** have died as a result of Chinese occupation since 1950 — through direct killing, famine caused by Chinese policies, torture in prisons, and forced labor. Even if this number is debated, scholars agree the death toll is **in the hundreds of thousands.**

---

### 🔫 1. Direct Military Killings (1950–1970s)
- When China invaded Tibet in 1950, the People's Liberation Army (PLA) killed thousands of Tibetan soldiers and civilians who resisted
- During the **1959 Lhasa Uprising**, Chinese forces opened fire on crowds — estimated **87,000 Tibetans killed** in Lhasa alone within 18 months, according to a captured PLA document
- Villages suspected of sheltering resistance fighters were **bombed and burned**
- Monks were executed publicly, monasteries were destroyed

---

### ☠️ 2. Deaths from Famine (1959–1962)
- Chinese agricultural collectivization policies destroyed Tibet's traditional farming and herding systems
- This caused a **massive famine** across Tibet
- Tens of thousands of Tibetans starved to death while China continued exporting food from the region

---

### 🏛️ 3. Cultural Revolution Killings (1966–1976)
- During China's Cultural Revolution, Tibet suffered enormously
- Over **6,000 monasteries** were destroyed
- Monks, nuns, and religious leaders were publicly tortured and executed
- Tibetans were forced to denounce and even **kill their own family members** in "struggle sessions"
- Anyone who resisted was shot or sent to labor camps where many died

---

### 🪖 4. 2008 Crackdown — Most Recent Mass Killing
- In March 2008, Tibetans rose up in the largest protests since 1959
- Chinese security forces responded with **live ammunition**
- **At least 80–140 civilians were killed** according to exile groups and witnesses
- Hundreds more were arrested, tortured, and some died in custody
- China sealed off Tibet entirely — no foreign journalists were allowed in

---

### 🔥 5. Self-Immolations — Deaths as Protest (2009–Present)
This is a uniquely tragic chapter:
- Since 2009, **over 160 Tibetans** have set themselves on fire to protest Chinese rule
- These include monks, nuns, students, and ordinary citizens
- Most died from their burns
- China arrested family members of those who self-immolated and banned mourning
- The UN and human rights groups blame Chinese repression as the direct cause

---

### ⛓️ 6. Deaths in Prison & Custody (Ongoing)
- Thousands of Tibetans have been arrested for peaceful activities — praying, owning a photo of the Dalai Lama, making a phone call, singing a song
- Many have **died in prison** from torture, starvation, denial of medical care
- Recent documented case: **Monk Phende Gyaltsen** — arrested in 2022 for renovating a monastery, died in prison January 2023 in good health before arrest
- **Gonpo Namgyal**, a language rights activist, died from torture in custody in recent years

---

### 📋 What Human Rights Organizations Say
- **Human Rights Watch**, **Amnesty International**, and the **UN Human Rights Council** have all confirmed killings, torture-related deaths, and forced disappearances in Tibet
- The **U.S. State Department** officially classifies Tibet as one of the worst human rights situations on Earth
- Freedom House rates Tibet **0 out of 100** — the lowest possible score, meaning zero freedom

### ⚠️ Critical Warning First
It is virtually impossible to determine the exact number of Tibetans arrested, dead, sentenced, or missing in Tibet considering the tremendous lack of transparency and severe restrictions on information flow. The actual figures are believed to be much higher than what is reported.

### 🔢 Estimated Total Deaths 2020–2026

The U.S. State Department confirmed reports of individuals dying in custody after being beaten, and multiple individuals who had been released from detention dying as a result of long-term illnesses and injuries suffered following beatings and mistreatment during incarceration.

Here is an honest breakdown of estimates:

**Deaths confirmed by name:** ~10–15 documented cases (2020–2025)

**Estimated actual deaths** (including unreported):
- Human rights organizations estimate **dozens to low hundreds** of deaths per year inside Tibet from torture, medical neglect, and prison abuse
- From 2020–2026 the realistic estimate is **somewhere between 200–600+ deaths** from custody-related causes
- This is part of a long-running pattern — in 2015 alone, the International Campaign for Tibet documented 29 Tibetans who suffered torture and mistreatment while in custody, many of whom died as a result.

---

### ❓ Why Can't We Get Exact Numbers?

Due to the Chinese government's extreme restrictions on communications for minority populations, reports of cases rarely become known outside the country. Authorities also forced families to immediately cremate bodies — preventing autopsies and destroying evidence of torture.

---

### 🔴 Bottom Line

**No one knows the exact number** — and that is itself the result of China's deliberate strategy. China blocks journalists, restricts internet, forces quick cremations, and silences families. What we know for certain is:

- Deaths are **ongoing and systematic**
- They happen **inside prisons and hospitals**, not in open streets
- The real number is **far higher** than what is publicly confirmed
- Every major human rights organization — **Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, the U.S. State Department, and the UN** — confirms this pattern of killing continues to this day

## Why Countries Don't Oppose China Over Tibet

- China is the **largest trading partner** for over 120 countries
- Almost every country in the world buys from or sells to China
- Companies like Apple, Samsung, Toyota, Walmart — all depend on China
- **No country wants to lose billions in trade** over Tibet

> **When forced to choose between human rights and money — most governments choose money.**

---

### 🏦 2. China's Debt Trap — Belt & Road Initiative
- China has **loaned trillions of dollars** to developing countries through its Belt & Road Initiative
- Over 140 countries are part of this program
- Countries that owe China money **cannot afford to criticize it**
- Africa, South Asia, Southeast Asia, Latin America — all deeply financially tied to China
- China has used economic coercion as a tool — countries that criticize China over Tibet or other issues face trade restrictions, cancelled deals, and diplomatic punishment.

- China is one of **5 permanent members** of the UN Security Council

- Any formal UN action against China over Tibet is **dead before it starts**
- China also controls large blocs of votes in UN agencies through its financial influence
