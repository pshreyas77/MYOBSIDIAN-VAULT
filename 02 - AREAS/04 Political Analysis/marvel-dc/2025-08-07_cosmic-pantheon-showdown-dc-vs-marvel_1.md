This analysis presents a fascinating theoretical battle between two of comics' most powerful cosmologies, and I can see you've put considerable thought into the power scaling involved. Let me help break down the core concepts and reasoning behind this matchup to deepen our understanding of how these cosmic hierarchies work.

The fundamental insight in your analysis centers on a crucial distinction in comic book cosmology: the difference between being extremely powerful within a reality system versus having the power to manipulate the very foundations that define what reality means. Think of it like the difference between being the strongest person in a video game versus being the programmer who can rewrite the game's code entirely.

When you mention Marvel fielding "more multiversal-level abstracts," you're identifying an important pattern. Marvel's cosmic entities like Eternity, Infinity, and the Celestials operate on truly staggering scales - they can manipulate entire universes, timelines, and dimensions. However, they still function within the established rules of Marvel's cosmological framework. They're incredibly powerful players in the game, but they're still playing by the game's rules.

DC's upper tier entities, particularly Lucifer and Michael, operate on what we might call a "meta-conceptual" level. When you suggest Lucifer could "remove the concept of Marvel continuity," you're pointing to their ability to manipulate the very ideas that define existence itself. This isn't just about raw power - it's about operating at a level of reality that's more fundamental than physical laws or even abstract concepts like space and time.

The Presence and The One Above All represent an interesting philosophical problem in these discussions. Both are positioned as essentially omnipotent within their respective fictional frameworks. When two truly omnipotent beings meet, we encounter what philosophers call the "omnipotence paradox" - can an all-powerful being create something that even they cannot destroy? Your conclusion of an "eternal stalemate" elegantly sidesteps this paradox by recognizing that true omnipotence, by definition, cannot be meaningfully threatened by another truly omnipotent force.

The 7-8 out of 10 odds you assign to DC reflect this conceptual advantage. It's not that DC's characters are necessarily "stronger" in raw power terms, but rather that they operate at a level of reality that's more fundamental than where Marvel's cosmic entities function.

What makes this analysis particularly compelling is how it mirrors real philosophical debates about the nature of existence and reality. Are there levels of being that are more "real" than others? Can concepts exist independently of the realities they describe? These are questions that have occupied philosophers for millennia, and superhero comics provide an interesting fictional laboratory for exploring them.
