### 1. Archaeological Evidence: The Oldest Buddhist Structure (550 BCE)

The most significant evidence for Buddhism's early presence comes from the **Maya Devi Temple** at Lumbini (Nepal), the traditional birthplace of the Buddha.

- **The Discovery**: In 2013, an international team of archaeologists led by Durham University uncovered traces of a **wooden structure** beneath the existing brick temple. This structure dates from **550 BCE** .
- **Scientific Dating**: The dating was confirmed using radiocarbon and optically stimulated luminescence techniques on charcoal from postholes and soil samples .
- **Nature of the Structure**: The excavation revealed a pattern of postholes indicating a wooden railing surrounding a tree shrine (*bodhigara*) with a central, unroofed space containing mineralized tree roots. The clay floors were worn smooth by visitors, confirming it was a place of worship. Critically, the site showed **no evidence of animal sacrifice**, aligning with the Buddhist principle of *ahimsa* (non-violence) .
- **Continuity of Worship**: The later brick temple was built directly over this ancient wooden shrine, preserving its layout. This demonstrates a continuity of Buddhist worship at the site for over two millennia .

**Conclusion**: This discovery pushes back the date of Buddhist architecture to the **6th century BCE**, making it the oldest Buddhist shrine in the world. It predates the oldest surviving Hindu stone temple by over 600 years .

### 2. Archaeological Evidence: Hindu Temples Built Over Buddhist Structures

Multiple excavations provide physical proof of Hindu temples being constructed directly on top of Buddhist sites, indicating a physical and historical succession.

- **Dinajpur, Bangladesh (Itakura Dhibi)** : An archaeological team from Jahangirnagar University discovered a **Hindu temple built directly over a Buddhist temple**. The Buddhist temple, dating to the **8th-11th century CE**, was deliberately demolished. Builders then constructed a Hindu temple on the same site, placing the *garbhagriha* (inner sanctum) **directly on top of the Buddhist *garbhagriha***. A 30-40 cm thick metal panel was used to separate the Buddhist base from the new Hindu structure. Remains of 13 Buddhist stupas, some containing cremated remains, were also found on the site .
- **Sravasti, Uttar Pradesh (Kachchi Kuti)** : The earliest structures at this site are Buddhist and date to the **Kushan period (1st-3rd century CE)** . A Brahmanical (Hindu) temple with terracotta panels depicting scenes from the *Ramayana* was constructed over these Buddhist remains during the **Gupta period (4th-6th century CE)** .
- **Badrinath Temple, Uttarakhand**: Historical records state that this temple was originally a **Buddhist shrine** until the **8th century CE**, when the Hindu philosopher Adi Shankara converted it into a Hindu temple .

**Conclusion**: These sites provide direct, stratified archaeological evidence of Buddhist structures being converted into, or replaced by, Hindu temples. This was not merely a change in patronage but a physical transformation of sacred spaces.

### 3. Textual Evidence: The Buddha Avatar in Hindu Scriptures

The incorporation of the Buddha into the Hindu pantheon as an avatar of Vishnu is a well-documented, **late development**.

- **First Appearance**: According to scholar Wendy Doniger, the myth of the Buddha as an avatar of Vishnu first appeared in the **Vishnu Purana** between **400 and 500 CE** . This is **900 to 1,000 years after the Buddha's life** .
- **The Purpose**: The Puranas describe this incarnation as a strategic move by Vishnu. The *Agni Purana* states Vishnu took the form of Buddha to **"delude the asuras" (demons)** , teaching them false doctrines so they would abandon the Vedas and be defeated . This presents the Buddha as a figure who misleads the unworthy, subordinating Buddhism within a Hindu framework.
- **Regional Variation**: While the *Bhagavata Purana* (c. 9th-10th century CE) includes the Buddha in the standard list of ten avatars (*Dashavatara*), he is **rarely worshipped** by Hindus in the same way as Rama or Krishna .

**Conclusion**: The identification of the Buddha as a Hindu avatar is a **late Puranic invention**, created centuries after his death, which served to incorporate a successful rival religion into the Hindu fold.

### 4. Evidence of Knowledge Destruction: The Burning of Nalanda

The destruction of key Buddhist institutions explains why some historical records are incomplete and why Buddhism declined in its land of origin.

- **Nalanda Mahavihara**: This was one of the world's first great universities, a center of Buddhist learning that attracted scholars from China, Korea, Tibet, and Southeast Asia .
- **The Library - Dharmaganja**: The complex housed a vast library known as **Dharmaganja** (Treasury of Knowledge), which consisted of three main buildings: *Ratnasagara*, *Ratnodadhi*, and *Ratnaranjaka* .
- **The Destruction**: In **1193 CE**, the university was sacked by Turkic invader Bakhtiyar Khilji. The library was so vast that it is said to have burned for **three months** .

**Conclusion**: The destruction of Nalanda represents an **irrecoverable loss of Buddhist knowledge**. Countless manuscripts on philosophy, science, history, and Buddhist doctrine were lost, making it more difficult to trace the full scope of Buddhism's early history and influence.

### 5. Counter-Evidence & Scholarly Debate: Coexistence and Preservation

While the evidence above shows a clear historical sequence, it is important to note that the relationship was not uniformly adversarial. Some scholars emphasize coexistence.

- **Preservation of Buddhist Sites by Hindu Kings**: The **Shunga dynasty**, often cited as "anti-Buddhist," actually **renovated and protected** Buddhist sites. The famous Sanchi Stupa was encased in stone and its *vedika* (railing) and *pradaksinapath* (circumambulatory path) were built during the Shunga period. The elaborately carved *toranas* (gateways) were added later by the Satavahana kings, who were also Vedic followers .
- **Coexistence at Sanchi**: At Sanchi, the main Buddhist stupa stands near **Temple No. 17**, one of the earliest examples of a Hindu temple, built during the Gupta period. This physical proximity suggests a period of coexistence and shared sacred space .

**Note on Interpretation**: This evidence suggests that while there was competition and conversion, there was also a history of patronage and co-existence. The physical conversion of Buddhist sites into Hindu temples, however, remains an archaeologically documented fact .
