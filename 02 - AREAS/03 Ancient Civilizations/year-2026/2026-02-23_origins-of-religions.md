Religion predates Göbekli Tepe, with archaeological evidence of ritual and symbolic practices appearing tens of thousands of years earlier during the Paleolithic era.[1][2]

## Göbekli Tepe Context
Göbekli Tepe, dated to 9500–8000 BCE (about 11,500–10,000 years old), features monumental T-shaped pillars interpreted as a ritual or temple site built by hunter-gatherers. It challenges the idea that organized religion arose only after agriculture but is not the earliest evidence.[3][4][5]

## Earliest Religion Evidence
Possible ritual offerings to a stone snake in South Africa date to 70,000 years ago, linked to early symbolic behavior. More confirmed sites include a 35,000–37,000-year-old ritual chamber in Manot Cave, Israel, with engravings and fire use for gatherings. These Paleolithic finds suggest animism or shamanism-like beliefs long before Neolithic monuments like Göbekli Tepe.[2][6][7][8][1]

## Key Comparison
| Aspect | Religion Evidence | Göbekli Tepe |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|
| Age | 70,000+ years ago (e.g., Python Cave rituals)[2] | ~11,500 years ago[5] |
| Type | Offerings, burials, cave art (Paleolithic)[7][8] | Monumental enclosures, carvings (Neolithic)[3] |
| Society | Hunter-gatherers, symbolic acts[1] | Pre-agricultural communal rituals[9] |
