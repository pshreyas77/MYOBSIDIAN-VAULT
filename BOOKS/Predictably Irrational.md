# Predictably Irrational

**Author:** Dan Ariely  
**Published:** 2008  
**Genre:** Psychology / Behavioral Economics  
**Pages:** ~280  

## Why It Matters

Ariely's experiments at MIT revealed that irrationality is not random — it follows predictable patterns. This challenges classical economics' assumption of rational actors and offers a framework for understanding human decision-making in everything from pricing to relationships.

## Key Themes

- **The Power of Price**: How price anchors shape perception of value
- **The Cost of Zero**: Why "free" is disproportionately attractive
- **Relativity**: How options are presented changes choice — not the choice itself
- **Social Norms vs. Market Norms**: Why paying for dinner changes a date's meaning
- **Procrastination and Self-Control**: The planning fallacy and why we can't save for retirement

## Key Takeaways

1. **Humans are predictably irrational** — understanding the patterns is the path to better decisions
2. **Context shapes choices**: The same option presented differently leads to different decisions
3. **Free is a powerful persuader**: "Free" creates an irrational value perception
4. **Social norms trump economic ones**: Markets erode social bonds — understand when to use which
5. **Acknowledging irrationality is the first step to mitigating it**

## Notable Quotes

> "The idea that we make decisions "rationally" is a comforting myth. In reality, we are swayed by forces we don't understand and can't control."

> "Free is not just a price — it's a powerful emotional force that can override reason."
