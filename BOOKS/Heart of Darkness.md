> For future Claude: This note covers *Heart of Darkness* (1899) by Joseph Conrad, a seminal work of English literature that explores the horrors of European colonialism in Africa. Narrated by Charles Marlow, it delves into the barbarity of imperialism and the darkness within human nature.

# Heart of Darkness

**Author:** Joseph Conrad
**Published:** 1899
**Genre:** Fiction / Classics / Colonial Literature
**Pages:** ~72 (novella)

## The Core Thesis

Conrad exposes the brutal reality of European colonialism in the Congo Free State, revealing that the true "heart of darkness" lies not in the African jungle, but within the souls of the colonizers. The journey up the Congo River becomes a metaphorical descent into the depths of human depravity.

## Key Themes

- **Colonialism and Imperialism**: The hypocrisy and brutality of the "civilizing mission"
- **The Nature of Evil**: The capacity for darkness within every human heart
- **Madness**: Kurtz's descent into tyranny and insanity
- **Civilization vs. Savagery**: Questioning which is which
- **The Unreliable Narrator**: Marlow's subjective and biased perspective

## Key Takeaways

1. **Colonialism is a shame** — it degrades both the colonized and the colonizer
2. **The horror! The horror!** — Kurtz's last words reflect a recognition of his own capacity for evil
3. **Civilization is a veneer** — Conrad suggests savagery lies beneath the surface of all "civilized" men
4. **Ambiguity is intentional** — the novel resists easy moral judgments, forcing the reader to confront complexity

## Notable Quotes
