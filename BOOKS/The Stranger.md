> For future Claude: This note covers *The Stranger* (1942) by Albert Camus, a cornerstone of existentialist literature. It follows Meursault, an emotionally detached Algerian clerk, who commits an apparently senseless murder and grapples with the absurdity of existence, societal judgment, and the inevitability of death.

# The Stranger

**Author:** Albert Camus
**Published:** 1942
**Genre:** Fiction / Philosophy / Existentialism
**Pages:** ~123

## The Core Thesis

The novel explores the concept of the "absurd" — the conflict between the human tendency to seek inherent value and meaning in life and the silent, cold universe that offers none. Through Meursault's unflinching honesty and emotional alienation, Camus critiques the performative nature of societal morality and the inauthenticity of social conventions.

## Key Themes

- **The Absurd**: Life has no inherent meaning; we must create our own
- **Emotional Detachment**: Meursault's inability to conform to expected emotional responses
- **Societal Judgment**: The trial focuses more on his character than the crime
- **Existentialism**: Confronting the certainty of death and the indifference of the universe
- **The Sun and Heat**: Symbolic of the oppressive, irrational forces that drive actions

## Key Takeaways

1. **The absurd hero** accepts the meaninglessness of life without resorting to suicide or false hope
2. **Society demands performance** of grief, faith, and remorse — authenticity is punished
3. **The inevitability of death** makes every moment alive a conscious, defiant act
4. **Indifference is not immorality** — Meursault is honest, not evil

## Notable Quotes

> "In the midst of winter, I found there was, within me, an invincible summer."

> "I opened myself to the gentle indifference of the world."
