> For future Claude: This note covers *The Catcher in the Rye* (1951), a banned-literature classic that remains one of the most-challenged and most-taught novels in the English language. It is a bildungsroman narrated by a teenager, Holden Caulfield, whose voice redefined first-person fiction.

# The Catcher in the Rye

**Author:** J.D. Salinger  
**Published:** 1951  
**Genre:** Fiction / Bildungsroman  
**Pages:** ~234  

## Why It Was Banned

The novel has been banned and challenged in schools and libraries for decades, primarily for:
- Profanity and coarse language (as per 1950s standards)
- Sexual content and references to prostitution
- Undermining of family values and authority
- Alleged promotion of rebellion and mental health instability (as per 1950s-60s moral panic)

As of 2026-06, it remains one of the most banned books in American history. However, its literary merit as a critique of adolescent alienation is well-established.

## Key Themes

- **Alienation**: Holden's inability to connect with others in a world he sees as "phony"
- **Loss of innocence**: The title metaphor — wanting to "catch" children before they fall off a cliff into adulthood
- **Identity and belonging**: The search for authentic connection in a superficial world
- **[[Teenage Rebellion]]**: A touchstone for adolescent literature ever since

## Key Takeaways

1. **Authenticity is rare**: Holden's voice is one of the most authentic in fiction — cynical, vulnerable, and deeply human
2. **The gap between appearance and reality is a recurring theme**
3. **First-person narrative can be the entire engine of a novel**
4. **Banned books often become the most influential**: Its suppression made it more popular
