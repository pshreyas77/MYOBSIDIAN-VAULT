> For future Claude: This note covers *Slaughterhouse-Five* (1969), one of the most banned books in the United States, by Kurt Vonnegut. It is an anti-war novel based on the author's experiences as a prisoner of war during the Dresden bombing. It remains one of the most taught and most challenged books in American schools.

# Slaughterhouse-Five

**Author:** Kurt Vonnegut  
**Published:** 1969  
**Genre:** Fiction / Anti-War / Science Fiction  
**Pages:** ~275  

## Why It Was Banned

- **Language and content**: Banned for profanity, sexual content, and violence (1982, Drake, North Dakota). Challenged in many school districts for "moral depravity."
- **Anti-war message**: Its explicit condemnation of war has made it controversial in conservative communities. Its depiction of the Dresden bombing as senseless violence was seen as unpatriotic.

## Key Themes

- **Trauma and time**: Billy Pilgrim's "unsticking" in time — post-traumatic stress as non-linear narrative
- **The absurdity of war**: The Dresden bombing as a meaningless, catastrophic event
- **Free will vs. determinism**: "So it goes" — the Tralfamadorians teach Billy that free will is an illusion
- **Science fiction as a vehicle for trauma**: Time travel and aliens as metaphors for dissociation

## Key Takeaways

1. **War is not glorious**: The book's central message is that war is senseless destruction
2. **Trauma can fracture time**: PTSD as the inability to escape the past
3. **"So it goes" is the novel's mantra**: Acceptance of death and loss as part of life
4. **Science fiction can be deeply serious**: Vonnegut proved the genre's capacity for profound meaning
