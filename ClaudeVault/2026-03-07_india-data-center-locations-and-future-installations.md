## 🇮🇳 India Data Center Map — Existing & Upcoming

### Key Statistics
As of 2024, India's data center capacity stands at 950 MW, expected to reach 1,800 MW by 2026. There are over 150 data centers in India, with 60% concentrated in Navi Mumbai, Chennai, Noida, Gurgaon, Bengaluru, and Hyderabad.

---

### 📍 Hub-by-Hub Breakdown

**🔴 Mumbai / Navi Mumbai — #1 Hub (52% of capacity)**
Maharashtra hosts approximately 85 data centers, the largest number in India. Mumbai and Navi Mumbai benefit from numerous submarine cable landings that enhance international connectivity, with companies like Yotta, AdaniConneX, and STT GDC concentrating investments here. Navi Mumbai has emerged as the third-biggest data center market in the Asia-Pacific region, with total capacity at 2,337 MW, breaching the 2 GW mark in 2023, behind Shanghai and Tokyo. Google is building an 8-storey, 381,000 sq ft data center in Navi Mumbai in partnership with Raiden Infotech.

**🟠 Chennai — #2 Hub (21% of capacity)**
Tamil Nadu is positioned in second place, with Chennai home to around 35 data centers, bolstered by cable landings and a growing IT ecosystem linked to the automotive and manufacturing sectors. Equinix opened its first AI-ready data center in Chennai on 19th September 2025 with a $69 million investment, connected to three other data centers in Mumbai.

**🟡 Hyderabad — Major & Growing Hub**
Hyderabad holds 6% of the market share in the data center industry. Two massive projects are underway: Microsoft's Hyderabad-based India South Central data center is set to go live in mid-2026 as its largest hyperscale region in the country, comprising three availability zones. AWS is investing $7 billion in a new cloud region in Hyderabad. STT GDC signed an MoU with the Telangana government to develop a data center at Mucherla in Hyderabad with a $412 million investment.

**🟢 Bengaluru — India's Silicon Valley (~30 DCs)**
Karnataka boasts 30 data centers, benefiting from its well-established IT talent pool and vibrant startup ecosystem, with firms like NTT and CtrlS flourishing in this environment. NTT announced a new "Bengaluru 4" campus spanning 8.5 acres with an INR 2,400 crore investment — a 100 MW facility.

**🔵 Noida / Delhi NCR (~25 DCs)**
Noida, located in Uttar Pradesh, benefits from its closeness to the Delhi-NCR region, hosting approximately 25 facilities, with companies like Yotta experiencing rapid expansion.

## ⚠️ Major Drawbacks of Data Centers in India

### 💧 1. Massive Water Consumption Crisis

This is arguably the most severe issue. A single 100 MW data center requires approximately 2 million liters of water daily for cooling — equivalent to the daily water needs of about 6,500 households.

India's data centers are expected to more than double their water use — from 150 billion liters in 2025 to 358 billion liters by 2030. An S&P Global study predicts that 60–80% of India's data centers will face high water stress this decade.

The problem is location-specific too. Mumbai delivers just over 3,000 million liters of water daily against much higher demand. In Bengaluru, the rural district is already extracting groundwater at 169% of permissible limits — officially classified as "over-exploited" since 2013.

The main culprit is evaporative cooling — data centers in India use this method because of the high temperatures, but approximately 80% of the water taken in simply evaporates and is permanently lost.

---

### ⚡ 2. Enormous & Fossil-Fuel-Heavy Power Demand

In India, data center expansion is projected to increase electricity demand nearly fivefold by 2030 — from ~13 TWh in 2024 to ~57 TWh.

Fossil fuels currently contribute close to 71–72% of total energy generation in India, meaning data centers will indirectly contribute to additional coal capacity since they need 24/7 energy. Renewables are critical for sustainability but are intermittent by nature and cannot yet guarantee round-the-clock supply.

---

### 🌍 3. Land Use & Local Community Pressure

Beyond energy, data centers consume large volumes of water and strain land, exerting significant pressure on local communities. The global data center industry currently consumes around 560 billion liters of water annually — equivalent to the domestic water demand of a city with roughly 8 million residents.

Grid-scale renewable energy will increasingly face land acquisition challenges, land-use conflicts, and extreme climate events, making sustainable expansion harder.

---

### 📊 4. Lack of Transparency & Accountability

India does not have comprehensive regulatory frameworks forcing data center operators to disclose water consumption.

Companies like AdaniConneX have no standalone ESG reports. Adani Enterprises reported a 59% increase in total water withdrawals from 2022 to 2025 — but lumps data centers together with mining, airports, and other businesses, making it impossible to isolate DC-specific impact. Several operators claim to be "water neutral" with little independent verification.

---

### 🏙️ 5. Geographic Concentration Risk

More than half of India's data center capacity is concentrated in Mumbai alone, with 15–18% in Chennai. These are cities already struggling with water shortages. This heavy clustering means that a single natural disaster, grid failure, or water crisis in Mumbai could disrupt a huge portion of India's digital infrastructure.

---

### 🔌 6. Grid & Transmission Bottlenecks

India already faces significant challenges across the renewable energy value chain — including grid integration constraints such as transmission capacity bottlenecks — and a lack of long-term power offtake agreements, making reliable 24/7 green power difficult to secure for data centers.

---

### 🌡️ 7. Heat & Cooling Technology Gap

Traditional air cooling can no longer efficiently handle the escalating heat produced by ever-increasing power densities of modern AI processors. Liquid immersion cooling is a superior solution but has significantly higher upfront costs than conventional methods.

---

In summary, India's data center boom is economically exciting but comes with serious environmental, infrastructural, and governance challenges — particularly around water scarcity and fossil fuel dependency — that need urgent policy attention.

## ✅ Solutions to India's Data Center Problems

---

### 💧 Problem 1: Water Overconsumption → **Smarter Cooling Technologies**

The biggest fix is moving away from traditional evaporative cooling to next-generation alternatives:

**Liquid Immersion Cooling** is the most powerful solution. Liquid immersion cooling involves submerging servers in a non-conductive liquid that absorbs heat directly. Compared to traditional methods, this can reduce water consumption by up to 91%, energy consumption by 50%, and space occupancy by 85%.

**Direct-to-Chip Cooling** is another innovation. It circulates dielectric coolant across the processor's cold plate, absorbing heat right at the source, minimizing overheating and excessive water use in the cooling process.

**Closed-Loop Cooling Systems** allow water to be reused repeatedly. Closed-loop cooling systems enable the reuse of both recycled wastewater and freshwater, allowing water supplies to be used multiple times. A cooling tower uses external air to return water to its original temperature, reducing freshwater use by up to 70%.

**Recycled & Non-Potable Water** should replace municipal water. Developers can use non-potable water sources such as recycled wastewater and captured rainwater to meet cooling needs — this is a well-established practice to conserve potable water, particularly in dry or drought-prone regions.

---

### ⚡ Problem 2: Fossil Fuel Dependency → **Renewable Energy Integration**

CtrlS is working towards a 100% renewable energy goal, achieving industry-lowest PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness), and is on a path to net-zero by 2040 — 30 years ahead of India's national commitment.

In September 2025, TERI and the National Solar Energy Federation of India (NSEFI) signed a landmark pact to tackle the carbon footprint of digital infrastructure, with a focus on integrating solar power with battery storage so that even when the sun isn't shining, facilities remain powered by clean energy.

---

### 🏗️ Problem 3: Geographic Concentration → **Smart Site Selection**

One of the seven viable plans recommended by researchers is deliberate site selection — placing new data centers away from water-stressed metros and toward regions with naturally cooler climates, abundant renewable energy, and lower water stress.

Andhra Pradesh is already promoting seawater cooling for coastal data centers, leveraging natural geography to reduce cooling-related electricity demand — a model other coastal states could replicate.

---

### 📋 Problem 4: Lack of Transparency → **Mandatory Reporting Standards**

Yotta Data Services has called for the national Data Centre Policy to set clear standards for non-potable water usage, wastewater recycling, and cooling efficiency, ensuring capacity growth does not come at the cost of community water security.

Rajasthan's 2025 policy is India's most comprehensive sustainability-oriented data center framework, integrating mandatory wastewater recycling, zero liquid discharge (ZLD), rainwater harvesting, groundwater recharge, advanced water management systems, and green building standards — positioning it as a reference model for all Indian states.

---

### 🏛️ Problem 5: Weak Policy → **Government Incentives for Green DCs**

India's Draft National Data Centre Policy 2025 (MeitY) proposes 20-year tax exemptions, GST input credits, and streamlined approvals specifically for green-certified facilities — financially rewarding operators who adopt sustainable practices.

The Green Building Council's LEED certification framework evaluates facilities on reduction in energy and water consumption, improved air quality, and safety standards. CtrlS's Mumbai data center became the world's first LEED Platinum-certified data center as a result of these efforts.

---

### 🤖 Problem 6: AI-Driven Energy Surge → **AI-Optimized Efficiency**

Data centers need to use advanced AI technologies to optimize cooling systems by analyzing patterns in energy usage and adjusting temperatures accordingly — identifying opportunities to reduce energy consumption during periods of lower data activity to further minimize waste.

The move toward modular infrastructure — where you build only as much as you need — combined with real-time power monitoring, ensures that every watt is accounted for, and facilities can scale sustainably rather than overbuilding from the start.

---

### 🤝 Problem 7: Community Impact → **Community Benefit Agreements**

Researchers recommend establishing Community Benefits Agreements (CBAs) — formal contracts between data center developers and local communities that guarantee jobs, infrastructure investment, and water security protections in exchange for approvals. Implementing projects in phases rather than all at once also allows communities and ecosystems time to adapt.

---

### 🌊 Bonus: The WEF's Circular Water Vision

A shift from incremental changes to comprehensive, circular water strategies built into digital infrastructure design is needed — not just for compliance, but as a strategic necessity. Hyperscale operators, technology providers, startups, and local communities must move beyond isolated pilots and work together in innovation ecosystems where shared infrastructure, knowledge, and investment can accelerate adoption.

---

In short, the solutions exist — the challenge is speed of adoption and regulatory enforcement. India has a rare opportunity to leapfrog older, wasteful models and set a global benchmark for sustainable digital infrastructure.
