# C. Natesa Mudaliar

## For future Claude
C. Natesa Mudaliar (1878–1936) was the third co-founder of the [[Justice Party]], handling finance and administration with press/media connections. From the Tamil Vellala community, he provided the practical logistics and infrastructure. Died in 1936 — before Periyar's takeover the following year transformed the party's ideology. Part of the founding troika with P. Theagaraya Chetty and T. M. Nair.

---

## Overview
**C. Natesa Mudaliar** was a Tamil Vellala businessman and the third co-founder of the [[Justice Party]]. He handled finance, administration, and press connections — the practical infrastructure that allowed the party to function. Died in 1936, before Periyar's radical transformation of the party's ideology in 1938.

## Primary Contributions

### Finance and Administration
Natesa Mudaliar managed the party's finances and day-to-day administration — the unglamorous but essential work of keeping an organization solvent and operational. His business acumen kept the party funded and functioning.

### Press and Media Connections
His press and media connections were crucial for propagating the Justice Party's message across Madras Presidency. Before modern mass media, newspaper access determined political reach. Natesa Mudaliar's network provided that reach.

## Relationship with Co-Founders
- **P. Theagaraya Chetty**: First president; organizational builder — shared business background and community leadership
- **T. M. Nair**: Co-founder; medical doctor; named the party — intellectual complement to Natesa Mudaliar's administrative pragmatism

Together, the three founders provided: organizational leadership (Theagaraya Chetty), intellectual framing (Nair), and administrative infrastructure (Natesa Mudaliar).

## Historical Context
Died in 1936 — one year before the party split of 1937 and two years before Periyar joined in 1938. His contribution was purely organizational/administrative. The ideological transformation (rationalist critique of Hinduism, women's rights, Dravidian identity as a mass movement) was entirely Periyar's contribution after Natesa Mudaliar's death.
