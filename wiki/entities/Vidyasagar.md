# [[Vidyasagar]]

## Overview
Indian educator and social reformer who played a key role in promoting women's education and widow remarriage in 19th century Bengal. He challenged Brahminical orthodoxy and worked for the upliftment of marginalized sections of society through educational and social reforms.

## Key Attributes
- Born: September 26, 1820
- Died: July 29, 1891 (aged 70)
- Nationality: Indian
- Ideology: Social reform, Humanism, Rationalism, Feminism
- Known for: Promotion of women's education, Advocacy for widow remarriage, Opposition to polygamy and child marriage

## Relationships
- Related to: [[Jyotirao Phule]] - Fellow social reformer with different regional focus
- Related to: [[Periyar E. V. Ramasamy]] - Fellow rationalist and social reformer
- Related to: [[B. R. Ambedkar]] - Later reformer who continued anti-caste struggle
- Related to: [[Savitribai Phule]] - Pioneer of women's education
- Part of: [[Bengal Renaissance]], [[Social Reform Movement in India]]
- Influenced by: Traditional Sanskrit education, experience of social inequities, rationalist thought
- Influenced: Women's education movement, Widow remarriage advocacy, Modern Indian education system

## Major Works and Contributions
- **"Barnaparichay" (1855)** - Bengali primer still used in primary education
- **"Hindu Bahubivah"** - Work advocating restriction of polygamy
- **"Bidhaba Vivah"** - Work advocating for widow remarriage
- **"Baliyadan"** - Work opposing child marriage
- Numerous textbooks on Sanskrit grammar, literature, and philosophy

## Historical Contributions
- **Women's Education**: Instrumental in establishing girls' schools in Bengal
- **Widow Remarriage**: Led movement that resulted in Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act, 1856
- **Educational Reform**: Modernized Sanskrit education, introduced western subjects
- **Social Reform**: Worked against child marriage, polygamy, and caste prejudices
- **Institutional Building**: Founded institutions like Metropolitan Institute, Calcutta Training School
- **Literary Contributions**: Wrote extensively to make education accessible
- **Administrative Role**: Served as Inspector of Schools, contributed to educational policy

## Philosophical Contributions
- **Rationalist Approach**: Applied reason to social problems rather than accepting tradition blindly
- **Humanist Ethics**: Emphasis on human welfare and dignity as basis for social reform
- **Critique of Orthodoxy**: Challenged religious sanctification of social inequalities
- **Egalitarian Vision**: Advocated for equal educational opportunities regardless of gender or caste
- **Scientific Temperament**: Promoted scientific thinking in education and social reform

## Legacy
- **Educational Legacy**: Revolutionized Bengali education and textbook production
- **Women's Rights Legacy**: Paved the way for women's educational opportunities in India
- **Social Reform Legacy**: Influenced later reform movements and legislation
- **Literary Legacy**: Contributed significantly to Bengali language and literature
- **Institutional Legacy**: Institutions founded continue to contribute to education
- **Cultural Impact**: Remembered as "Dayar Sagar" (ocean of kindness) for his humanitarian work

## Notes
- Born into a poor Brahmin family in Birsingha, Midnapore (now West Bengal)
- Given title "Vidyasagar" (ocean of learning) by Sanskrit College, Calcutta
- Served as principal of Sanskrit College from 1851-1858
- Faced opposition from orthodox Hindu groups for his reformist activities
- Advocated for education of women and lower castes despite societal norms
- His campaign for widow remarriage faced severe backlash but ultimately succeeded
- Continued social reform work until his death in 1891
- His writings continue to be used in educational contexts across Bengal
